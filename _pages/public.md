My research addresses real-world problems — water scarcity, climate change, food systems, and poverty — that affect millions of people in developing countries. Below I summarize the key findings from my work in plain language.

---

## When your neighbors save water, so do you

Governments often run campaigns to encourage people to save water. But do these campaigns spread beyond the households they target? In a field experiment in Colombia, I found that households *not* directly targeted by a water-saving campaign still reduced their consumption — simply because their neighbors were targeted. Moreover, the more neighbors who received the campaign, the stronger the effect on directly treated households. This suggests that social influence is a powerful and underutilized tool in environmental policy: well-designed campaigns can produce effects that are larger than their direct reach.

[Read the full paper](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.reseneeco.2024.101463)

## Fines and droughts: does the threat of punishment get communities to save water?

When a drought is coming, can the threat of fines get people to cooperate and save water collectively? In Colombia, we studied communities facing the announcement of an El Niño drought event and a fine-based water policy. We find that the combination of an anticipated shock and regulatory threat affects collective conservation behavior — but the effects depend heavily on community characteristics and the credibility of enforcement.

[Working paper available here](https://www.josedavidlopezrivas.com/_files/ugd/1b970b_90847913fc4843b98045ae32c9b31c56.pdf)

## When rains fail, conflict rises

In pastoral communities in Kenya and Ethiopia, livestock raids — a form of violent conflict — are closely tied to the availability of pasture. We find that when rains fail and pasture becomes scarce, raids increase. But we also find a more surprising result: when pasture is abundant after a good rainy season, conflict also rises — as communities compete to access it. Drought insurance reduces conflict on average, but can have unintended effects depending on timing.

[Read the working paper](https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:ec327279-68d9-484a-a5f5-7bdecfa26def)

## How much is the ocean worth?

Coastal and marine ecosystems — coral reefs, mangroves, seagrasses — provide enormous benefits to humanity: food, tourism, coastal protection, and carbon storage. But these benefits are rarely reflected in economic decisions. We reviewed 67 scientific studies that have tried to put a dollar value on these services, finding wide variation depending on ecosystem type and location. The evidence strongly supports greater investment in marine conservation as a cost-effective climate strategy.

[Read the full paper](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.marpol.2024.106033)

## Carbon credits for small farmers in Tanzania

Can carbon markets help small farmers in developing countries while also protecting the environment? We are evaluating a pilot program in Tanzania where coffee farmers receive payments for the carbon stored in trees on their land. Companies buy these credits to offset their emissions. We are studying whether this arrangement improves farmers' incomes, changes how they manage their land, and delivers real environmental benefits.

*This project is ongoing. Results forthcoming.*

## Policy Implications

Across my research, several lessons emerge for policymakers designing behavioral and environmental interventions in low-income settings:

- **Social context matters.** Interventions that ignore how people influence each other systematically underestimate both their potential reach and their unintended consequences.
- **Spillovers are a resource.** Campaigns that spread through social networks can deliver effects well beyond their direct target — but this requires deliberate design.
- **Incentives interact with norms.** Market-based instruments like fines, carbon credits, or payments for ecosystem services work differently depending on whether they align with or cut against community norms and expectations.
- **Timing and anticipation matter.** Communities respond not just to shocks but to the *anticipation* of shocks — a finding with direct implications for climate adaptation policy.
