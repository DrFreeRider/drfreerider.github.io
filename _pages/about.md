I am an applied economist studying how social mechanisms shape the 
effectiveness of policy interventions in sustainability and development 
transitions. My research examines how information diffusion, peer effects, 
social norms, and reciprocity determine whether interventions — 
informational, regulatory, market-based, or financial — produce their 
intended effects in low-income settings.

Using field experiments, quasi-experimental methods, and discrete choice 
experiments across Colombia, Peru, Tanzania, and the Netherlands, I find 
that individual behavioral responses to policy are systematically 
conditioned by social context: who else is targeted, how information 
travels through communities, and whether interventions align with or cut 
against prevailing norms. These social dynamics operate across 
environmental, food system, and development domains, suggesting that 
accounting for them is a general condition for intervention effectiveness — 
not a context-specific nuance.

My work aims to improve the design of behavioral and development policy by 
identifying when and how social structure can be leveraged to amplify 
intended effects, and when it produces unintended ones.
